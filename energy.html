The Wind Turbine Design Project â€“ Phase 2 (EPRI_WIND2) is a senior design project that focuses on wind energy. UNC Charlotte has collaborated with the Electric Power Research Institute (EPRI) to design, assemble, and test a wind turbine that will compete in the 2022 U.S. Department of Energy collegiate wind turbine competition. This wind turbine will be designed and built within the rule-set created by the U.S. Department of Energy Collegiate Wind Competition. Since the end goal of the project is to compete in 2022, there are multiple senior design projects, with each having specific tasks to complete and hand off to the next team to prepare towards the competition (UNC Charlotte 2019). The first team, EPRI_WIND, was tasked with designing and creating a prototype wind turbine that will be tested in UNC Charlotte's wind tunnel. Our team, EPRI_WIND2, is tasked with completing three main tasks that will provide a holistic senior design experience and be essential for entry into the 2022 wind competition. These tasks are the Request for Proposal (RFP) document required to enter the competition, site analysis and design for a potential 100-MW wind farm in North Carolina, and a re-design of the wind turbine created by the EPRI_WIND team.

Design

Specifications for this design include a maximum dimension requirement of 42cm x 42cm x 42cm for the generation portion and 60cm by 15cm diameter tower. The turbine is expected to generate power at as little as 5m/s and withstand wind speeds of up to 25m/s. Analysis was done to determine the rotor tip speed at all the potential velocities. For the calculations, an understanding of fluid mechanics, aerodynamics, statics, and solids were essential in making assumptions of the forces being applied in critical areas.

Controls

The turbine design receives input from an encoder placed at the front of the generator to determine the RPM at any given moment of the output shaft. There is also an encoder attached to the stepper motor which provides feedback for pitch control. Pitch is determined by the speed of the output shaft and is pitched closer to feathered as the wind speed becomes more volatile. The maximum RPM of the chosen generator is 5000rpm with a maximum power output of 48 volts.

Blade Element Rotor Theory

Air foil designs were obtained from the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics which provided essential data such as the lift/drag forces at different angles of attack. With the air foil data, I was able to scale up a chosen design and calculate the lift force at different segments of the rotor blade.

VOLT

Volt is a student lead/found organization, built with the intention of inspiring students to explore potential careers in the energy industry. Energy and power are an integral part of everyday life and there is growth and opportunities for people looking to be involved.

What We Do

So how do we "empower the energy and power professionals of tomorrow by offering them opportunities to broaden their horizons and enhance their skill sets today"?  Simple.  We focus on three key areas that industry members are looking for in potential employees, areas that either are not offered by the university or not consolidated in a single delivery mechanism.

Professional Development

Our professional development program seeks to enhance the services offered by the university as well as provide members-only access to industry professionals in order to begin to prepare students to be "real world ready" on Day 1 of their first job after graduation.  From resume writing seminars to industry-related career coaching seminars to networking events with industry members, the students of VOLT have a world of opportunities to enhance the

Duke Energy

2019

My Experience

Worked as a reliability engineer during the summer. Spent much of my time observing power outages, communicating with linemen, and working at different operational centers throughout the Carolinas. I had the opportunity to reverse engineer the power grid system and observe various departments throughout the company that were responsible for different operations and segments of the grid. Being at the level of distribution, I learned a lot about the safety components used to ensure grid safety during a disaster event and SMART technology that allows power to continue even after a power outage. I also found consumer relations to be important as Duke Energy promotes a positive brand that keeps the lights on and will continue to provide reliable and consistent power to its customers.
